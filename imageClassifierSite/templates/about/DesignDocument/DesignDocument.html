Executive Summary 

Physics is a field of study involving many concepts and subjects that are not intuitive and at some scales behaves in a way that is contradictory to our everyday experiences. Many people visit science centers, planetariums, and science museums to learn more about science, but several aspects of physics are not made readily accessible by these displays.

In order to empower these users (ages 10+) to learn about these exciting, but difficult physics concepts, we are building an augmented reality (AR) physics visualization software system that will be run on the Microsoft HoloLens. This visualization system will give users the ability to visualize and interact with principles of physics that can be difficult to see, demonstrate, and/or imagine. Specifically, we will create a suite of visualization modules that cover a wide range of physics principles. The four main modules will be: kinematics, particle and quantum physics, astronomy, and circuits. Using holograms and the AR capabilities provided by HoloLens, these modules will allow the user to interact with the system through rotating, scaling, and moving objects to better visualize the interactions. In addition to passively observing these systems, we will provide additional controls to allow users to modify certain parameters that will affect the interactions in the system.

To help ensure that the process of learning physics is enjoyable, we will add game elements, such as random environments and puzzles, objectives, and setting hit points (HP). We believe that this project will help users at all levels of physics knowledge gain a richer and deeper understanding of the principles of physics. This software can also be expanded to include other science majors in the future.

By helping both students and adults have positive experiences while learning science, our software can make an impact. As students learn more about science in a fun way, it can influence their decision when selecting their majors. Also, as parents’ attitudes towards science improve, it will impact the impression their children have towards science. This effect can come not only through parents, but also through other family members and friends. Our goal for this project is to help students consider STEM majors fairly and help everyone gain a greater knowledge of and appreciation for physics.

Background

Rationale

In the United States as of 2013 there were 394 science centers listed as members of the Association of Science-Technology Centers and it was estimated that 73 million people visit these museums per year.[1] Despite this prevalence of visiting science centers, the Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study (TIMSS) created an index to measure students’ attitudes towards science and collected data from more than 20 countries. A high score indicates that students have a positive attitude towards science and place a high value on it. The study found that in the US, only 47% of the students were in the high category, compared to the national averag of 57%.[2] The study also found that in 2003 only 35% of students agreed strongly that they “Enjoy learning science” compared to the international average of 44%.

In a study  from 2013, it was found that K-12 and post-secondary students in the US “lack interest in STEM relative to the societal and labor market demands. For example, many high school and postsecondary students divert from STEM pathways and occupations into other fields. More than three out of four high school students who test in the top mathematics quartile do not pursue a STEM major in college. Additionally, of those who pursue STEM majors, only 50% actually complete their degree in a STEM area.”[3]

Similar Ideas

Utilizing AR and virtual reality (VR) for education is a trend that is gaining a lot of momentum as these technologies mature. Many companies and open source projects have been launched to pursue this open field, but it is still early there is plenty of space for new entrants. Each of these projects has a specific niche that they are seeking to fill and our project will help to fill a gap that the current products do not meet.

Along with many other groups, our project seeks to help address the societal problem of students and adults lacking positive attitudes towards science, and particularly physics, by helping them to have a positive experience with science. Many of the top companies that are using AR and VR are focused on allowing people to tour physical locations that they may not otherwise be able to see, such as the colliseum, stonehenge, Mars, the International Space Station (ISS), the Great Barrier Reef, etc. Several others seek to provide additional digital content at physical locations, such as museums.[4]

Our project seeks to provide a combination of contextual data with a real-time immersive experience, allowing users to interact directly with principles of physics. By enabling the user to visualize velocities, accelerations, and forces in real time which would otherwise be invisible, we will be able to help users learn more about some of the fundamental concepts of physics through active participation.

Required Technology

For our project we are going to be using the HoloLens to develop a suite of 3D physics visualizations. The HoloLens requires the third update of Visual Studio 2015 and the Windows 10 SDK. For the 3D modeling we need the Unity for HoloLens, which is available for free. We will also be using a HoloLens headset, however, we only have one headset. In order for every team member to test the application we will also need to use the HoloLens emulator, which also comes free. The emulator will allow us to run Windows Holographic in a virtual machine without the HoloLens.

 

In our project we will be focusing on showcasing 3D physics modules, so instead of creating our own physics engine we are going to be using an existing physics engine. The physics engine that we have chosen to use in our project is the Farseer Physics Engine. Farseer meets our requirements and it has great documentation.

 

For the 3D modeling we will be using Maya and Unity to create our own models. Most of these models will be created from scratch, however, we must leverage the objects we can create in a timely manner, and 3D objects that we can use from pre-existing sources.  In the kinematics module we will be able to create most of our own models, since they’re quite simple, but for other modules that have more complex models such as the subatomic particles or in the astronomy module we will need to utilize some pre-existing models.

 

For the subatomic module, the idea is to have a simulation of the collision where the Higgs Boson particle was discovered. We will need data from collisions that resulted in the discovery and instead of running our own simulation, we are planning on obtaining the data of the collision and using that data for the simulation.

 

Finally, as mentioned earlier we will need to interact with the holograms, and for that we will need to use certain gestures. The gestures that we will be using are the current gestures that the HoloLens comes preloaded with.  At the moment there is no way to implement new gestures for the HoloLens, therefore we will have to work with the current limited gestures, but if that changes in the future we will look into implementing new custom features that will allow us to uniquely interact with our physics simulator.  

System Requirements

The Windows 10 SDK runs the best on Windows 10 operating system. However, the SDK is also supported on older operating systems, such as Windows 8.1, Windows 8, Windows 7, Windows Server 2012 and Windows Server 2008 R2.

 

The emulator in based on Hyper-V and it uses it for the installation, therefore the system must be able to support it. The emulator uses RemoteFx for hardware acceleration and in order to use the emulator the following hardware requirements must be met.

 

  • 64-bit Windows 10 Pro, Enterprise, or Education (The Home edition does not support Hyper-V or the HoloLens emulator)
  • 64-bit CPU
  • CPU with 4 cores (or multiple CPU's with a total of 4 cores)
  • 8 GB of RAM or more
  • In the BIOS, the following features must be supported and enabled:
  • Hardware-assisted virtualization
  • Second Level Address Translation (SLAT)
  • Hardware-based Data Execution Prevention (DEP)
  • GPU (The emulator might work with an unsupported GPU, but will be significantly slower)
  • DirectX 11.0 or later
  • WDDM 1.2 driver or later

Software/Hardware Requirements

In order to use our project, the user would need to have a Microsoft HoloLens.  Once the user does have that, they can go to the Windows Store, download our project and run it.  No other work is needed to get it working as our project is built specifically for HoloLens.

Requirements Analysis

System Architecture

Modules:

Our system is comprised primarily of three different modules: Astronomy, Kinematics, and Particle Physics. The user is able to access these modules from the main menu station. The user can also select the “Quit” button to close the program.

Scenes:

Each module is composed of multiple levels that are each scenes constructed in Unity. A scene contains 3D models of objects and C# scripts that are applied to them to produce specific actions (allowing a user to select them, applying velocity or acceleration, orbiting, collision detection, etc.).

Tutorial:

Our system presents the user with tutorials before each level of each module. These tutorials introduce the objects the user will be able to interact with, the functionality of each object, and the relevant principles from physics. Each tutorial is another Unity scene.  

Game:

Each module consists of various levels. At any point in time, the user can choose to exit this module to go back to the main menu station. When the user completes a level, they can progress to the next level or replay the level to start over from the beginning.

Calculate:

Once in the game the user can interact with different components in the game, in order to complete the objective of the level that he/she is currently on by applying a velocity vector to the main object (the avatar).  When the input has been set, and the user is satisfied with the inputs then the values will be computed to determine if he/she has passed the level. This computation is done dynamically within the physics engine classes that we have established and which utilize the built-in Unity physics engine. We have defined a C# abstract class physics engine which defines some key methods that all others will utilize. For each level, we have developed a specific physics engine which extends the abstract class and implements additional functionality specific to that level. The calculation of position and velocity are computed on the fly utilizing these physics engine scripts.

Results:

Finally the results will determine if the user can advance to the next level, or remain on the same level, and try changing the input values.

Software Engineering Tools and Techniques

Software Engineering Methodology - We will use a combination of traditional and Agile methodologies. The first half of the project has been focused on designing and scoping the project, similar to the traditional software engineering methodology. However, the remainder of the year will be broken into sprints that involve developing and testing.

Versioning - We will use a GitLab repository for our versioning system. Git is widely used both in industry and academia and is a good fit for our engineering team. Our branching strategy is that we will have a sprint branch for each of our development sprints and each team member will create feature branches off the sprint branch for the features that they work on. Before a feature branch is merged into the sprint branch, we will have code review with other team members. Code reviews will be performed at team meetings by having at least one other team member review the code. At the end of each sprint, the sprint branch will be merged into the master branch.

Bug Tracking - We will use GitLab’s issue tracker to manage our bug tracking. Using the various features that GitLab provides means that we are able to work within an integrated, consistent environment rather than managing our project across multiple systems.  

Testing - We will have unit tests using Visual Studio. As discussed above, we will perform code reviews before feature branches are checked into our sprint branches. Integration and regression testing will primarily be performed manually through the HoloLens emulator. Before each release, we will perform thorough manual testing by deploying our project to the HoloLens.

Documentation - To document our project we will create dedicated website. This website will have pages that provide a general overview, a more detailed description for users, and technical documentation for future developers who may become involved in the project. We will be keeping the technical documentation portion of the website updated throughout the project so that a new developer could easily be brought up to speed.

Team Communication - We will primarily communicate as a team using Slack and via in person meetings. Slack is a means of communication that is common in industry and academia. It has the additional advantage that most of our team has already used this technology.

Team Meetings - For the rest of this semester, our team meetings will continue to be held each week on Monday, Wednesday, Friday from 11:30 am - 12:30 pm. For the Spring semester, we will be meeintg primarily during and after class time.

Time Scale

Alpha Release

For the alpha release, we plan to accomplish the following features in addition to what we accomplished for our prototype: applying vectors to the main game object (the avatar), update to the calculation and display of vectors based on the applied vectors and trajectory, tutorials for early levels of all modules, new 3D models for particle physics module, levels 1 and 2 of the particle physics module, and the associated physics engine for this module.  

Beta Release

For the beta release, we plan to enhance the project by adding the following features: a menu that allows the user to toggle the display of various vectors, adding sound effects to actions of holograms, additional options for the appearance of the avatar, levels 3 and 4 of each module, ability of the user to pause the avatar in midflight and apply new vectors,

Production Release

For the production release we plan to finalize our project by adding the following features: 3D model and visualization of gravity as the bending of space-time, animation effects on objects (such as solar flares, etc.), animations for the annihilation of matter-antimatter collisions, and level 5 for each module.

Appendix: Use Cases

The uses cases for this project are divided into 4 categories: general use cases, particle physics, astronomy, and kinematics. The particle physics, astronomy, and kinematics use cases each explain how the user would interact with the module of the same name. The general use cases involve starting the app and selecting a module, exiting a module, and general actions that are used across different modules.

List of Steps

Use Case G3

Title

Apply a Vector

Short Description

How a user applies a vector (such as acceleration or velocity) to the avatar.

List of Steps
  1. The user gazes at the menu of available options and selects the type of vector to apply from the menu
  2. The user gazes at the avatar, does an air tap, and holds

  4. The user releases the air tap to finalize the vector
  5. Optionally, the user can gaze at the vector, air tap, and hold. Steps 3-4 are then repeated. Step 5 can be repeated indefinitely.
